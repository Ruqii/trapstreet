# Name Rationale: Why "Trap Street"

We considered three navigation/cartography-themed names and shipped Trap Street.
Here's the full story.

---

## The shortlist

| Candidate | Story | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Trap Street** | Fake street planted in a map to catch copycats | ✅ **Picked** — domain `trapstreet.run` registered |
| Rhumb | Constant-bearing line in marine navigation | Reserved as a metric term ("on-rhumb" / "off-rhumb") |
| Driftless | US Midwest region untouched by glaciers | Too many companies already use it |

---

## Why we landed on Trap Street

### 1. It maps directly to the product, not just the vibe

The other names evoke a quality (no drift, stable bearing). Trap Street **describes
a mechanism we are literally building.**

In the trust-tiered architecture (see [`trust-tiers.md`](trust-tiers.md)), one of
the core anti-cheat features is exactly this: **we seed verifiable falsehoods or
ground-truth probes inside our held-out task set.** AI tools that fabricate,
copy, or shortcut trip these traps. Tools that actually do the work pass through.

The product feature is called "trap street." The brand is called Trap Street.
The domain is `trapstreet.run`. The product, the brand, and the URL are the
same idea — that's the kind of alignment that makes brands stick.

### 2. The story tells itself in one sentence

> *Mapmakers planted fake streets on real maps to catch copycats. We do the
> same for AI workflows.*

That sentence is a 30-second pitch. A reporter can write the lede. A user can
explain the product to their boss. A meme account can turn it into a graphic.
**Brand-as-explanation is the cheapest distribution.**

Compare the other names:

- "Rhumb is a constant-bearing line a sailor holds without recalculating its
  heading…" — by sentence three, the listener is gone.
- "Driftless refers to a region of the US Midwest that escaped Ice Age
  glaciation…" — also gone.

### 3. It pairs with the H4 manifesto, doesn't replace it

Harrison's H4 is the **why we exist** story (no more drift, no more theories).
Trap Street is the **how we work** story (planted truths catch the fakes).
We get to keep both, layered.

The site copy can run:

> *In the 1750s, the clock that didn't drift was the H4. In the 1930s,
> mapmakers caught copycats with trap streets. We're putting both to work
> on AI workflows.*

That's a strong opening for a landing page.

### 4. Domain availability + TLD alignment

`trapstreet.run` is a perfect fit:
- The TLD `.run` matches the core product verb ("run an eval")
- Two words, one syllable each, easy to type
- Memorable visually: street + run = movement, action

Domains for the runner-up names were either taken, parked, or in fights with
existing brands.

### 5. It is its own marketing channel

The product has a signature mechanic — **trap streets planted in held-out tasks**.
That mechanic produces signature outputs:

- "Trap Street Wall" — a public failure gallery of tools that tripped the traps
- Daily card format: *"AI said X. The trap said Y."*
- Shareable: the trap-tripping screenshot is a self-contained story

The brand and the meme are the same word.

## What we keep from the runners-up

### Rhumb → metric vocabulary

We use "rhumb" as the **name of our drift metric**. A workflow's "rhumb stability"
is how consistently it scores across runs and model updates. "Off-rhumb" means
the workflow has drifted. The brand is Trap Street; the dialect borrows from
navigation.

### Driftless → status badge

Top-tier workflows that maintain Gold-tier scores across N consecutive monthly
evals earn the **Driftless badge** — borrowed from the geological term, awarded
to workflows that stay accurate over time. Aspirational, scarce, visually
distinct from the standard Gold ribbon.

That way every name we considered earns its keep somewhere in the system.

## Origin candidates (kept for the record)

### Trap Street — full etymology

A trap street is a fictitious street that mapmakers deliberately insert into a
published map to catch copyright infringement. If a competitor copies your map,
the fake street appears in their version too — instant proof of fraud. Also
called "paper towns" or "cartographic honeypots."

The most famous example is **Agloe, New York** — a paper town invented in the
1930s by the General Drafting Company. Decades later a general store opened on
the spot and named itself after the fake town on the map. The fiction became
real.

### Rhumb — full etymology

A rhumb line (also *loxodrome*) is a path on a sphere that crosses every meridian
of longitude at the same angle. On a flat Mercator projection map, a rhumb line
appears as a straight line; on a globe, it spirals.

Sailors used rhumb lines for centuries because they're the line you can actually
hold — a constant compass bearing, no recalculation, hour after hour. The
shortest path between two points on a globe is a great-circle route, but
great-circle routes require continuously adjusting heading. A rhumb line is
longer but stable, repeatable, humanly executable.

The word comes from Old French *rumb* and Spanish/Portuguese *rumbo*, meaning
"compass point." Same root as "rumba" (the dance) — both involve a steady,
repeating direction.

### Driftless — full etymology

The Driftless Area is a region of the upper Midwest of the United States — parts
of Wisconsin, Minnesota, Iowa, and Illinois — that was never covered by glaciers
during the last Ice Age. Surrounding regions were reshaped by *glacial drift*
(rock and sediment glaciers deposit). The Driftless Area escaped. It kept its
original topology — deep river valleys, exposed bedrock, no flat plains.
Geologists call it "driftless" because it has no drift deposits.
